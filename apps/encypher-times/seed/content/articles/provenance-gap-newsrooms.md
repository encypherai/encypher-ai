When Reuters photographer Adrees Latif captured a Pulitzer-winning image of a woman and child at the U.S.-Mexico border, the photograph carried his byline and copyright notice. Within 48 hours of publication, the image had been shared more than 2 million times across social media platforms. Fewer than 3 percent of those shares retained any attribution to Latif or Reuters.

This pattern -- professional content stripped of its provenance as it propagates through the digital ecosystem -- represents what media researchers call the "provenance gap." It is the technical and structural failure that enables misinformation, unauthorized AI training, and the systematic erosion of publisher economics.

"Every time content is copied, screenshotted, re-uploaded, or embedded, there's a chance the attribution gets lost," said Dr. Claire Wardle, co-founder of the Information Futures Lab at Brown University. "The internet was built for sharing, not for provenance. We're now seeing the consequences of that architectural decision."

The provenance gap affects every content type. Text articles are copied and pasted without links. Images are downloaded, cropped, and re-uploaded without metadata. Videos are screen-recorded and re-posted. At each step, the connection between the content and its creator weakens.

For newsrooms, the financial implications are staggering. The News Media Alliance estimates that the loss of attribution and the resulting inability to track content usage costs the U.S. publishing industry more than $12 billion annually in unrealized licensing revenue.

New cryptographic approaches are beginning to address the problem. Technologies that embed invisible, persistent markers directly into content -- rather than relying on easily stripped metadata fields -- offer a fundamentally different approach to provenance. These markers survive copying, screenshotting, and even AI processing, maintaining a verifiable chain of custody from creation to consumption.

"The old approach was to put a label on the outside of the envelope," explained Erik Svilich, CEO of Encypher. "Of course it falls off. The new approach puts the identity inside the content itself, at the molecular level. It can't be separated without destroying the content."

Several major wire services have begun pilot programs to embed cryptographic provenance in all distributed content. Early results show that provenance survival rates -- the percentage of content shares that retain verifiable attribution -- increase from less than 5 percent with traditional metadata to more than 92 percent with embedded cryptographic markers.
