**By: Erik Svilich, Founder & CEO | Encypher | C2PA Text Provenance Co-Chair**

The term "watermarking" appears in two distinct technical contexts that have almost nothing in common beyond the metaphor of invisible marks. Cryptographic watermarking embeds verifiable identity information into content at the time of creation. Statistical watermarking shifts the statistical distribution of AI-generated outputs so the model that created them can recognize them.

These approaches answer different questions for different audiences. Using them interchangeably in policy discussions or product evaluations produces confusion that leads to bad infrastructure decisions. This post defines both approaches precisely, explains what each can and cannot do, and describes when each is appropriate.

---

## Statistical Watermarking: The SynthID Model

Google DeepMind's SynthID is the most prominent example of statistical watermarking, and it is worth examining in detail to understand the approach.

SynthID works differently for different modalities. For text, the approach uses a technique called logit-biasing during inference. When a large language model generates text, it computes a probability distribution over its vocabulary at each token position, then samples from that distribution to choose the next token. The top candidates often have similar probabilities, meaning the model could plausibly choose any of several words without significantly affecting the quality of the output.

SynthID's text watermarking biases these choices at certain positions in a way that is correlated with a seed derived from the model's context. The bias is small enough that the resulting text is indistinguishable from unbiased output to a human reader. However, because the bias pattern is correlated with a specific seed, the originating model can detect whether a piece of text exhibits the expected pattern.

Detection works by running the same bias calculation on the text in question. If the text's token choices align with the expected bias pattern at a rate significantly above chance, the detector concludes the text was likely generated by the watermarked model.

For images, SynthID uses a different mechanism: a neural network adds imperceptible modifications to image pixel values during generation. A corresponding neural network detector checks for those modifications.

## What Statistical Watermarking Can and Cannot Do

Statistical watermarking has specific, well-defined capabilities and specific, well-defined limitations.

**It can do:**
- Allow the originating model to recognize its own outputs
- Scale to any volume of output without additional infrastructure
- Work transparently without requiring any action from users

**It cannot do:**
- Prove who asked the model to generate the content
- Identify the content's intended use or rights status
- Survive paraphrasing, translation, or substantial editing
- Establish ownership by a human creator
- Provide evidence that holds up outside of detection-as-inference

The fragility to paraphrasing is the most significant limitation for rights enforcement applications. If someone takes a SynthID-watermarked piece of text and rewrites it, changing half the words, the statistical pattern that SynthID embeds is likely destroyed. The detector will no longer recognize the text as originating from the watermarked model.

This is not a defect in SynthID's design. It is a consequence of the mechanism. The watermark is a statistical signature over token choices. Changing the token choices changes the signature. The approach works well for the use case it was designed for: detecting that a piece of text was recently generated by a specific model with minimal modification.

## Cryptographic Watermarking: The C2PA Model

[Cryptographic watermarking](/cryptographic-watermarking/vs-statistical-watermarking) works on a fundamentally different principle.

Rather than modifying the content generation process to embed a detectable statistical pattern, cryptographic watermarking embeds an explicit identity token into the content after it is created. The token is signed using a private key, producing a signature that can be verified against the corresponding public key. The token also includes a hash of the content itself, binding the signature to the specific content it was embedded in.

For text, Encypher's implementation uses Unicode variation selectors, invisible characters that render as zero-width glyphs but carry binary payload. The full C2PA manifest, including signer identity, content hash, timestamp, and assertions, is encoded in these characters and distributed throughout the text. The mechanism is defined in C2PA 2.3 Section A.7.

For images, the manifest is embedded in the file's JUMBF container structure. For audio and video, it uses format-appropriate metadata blocks. The content hash in the manifest covers the media content itself, so any alteration that changes the pixel data, audio samples, or character sequence invalidates the hash binding.

---

## What Cryptographic Watermarking Can and Cannot Do

**It can do:**
- Prove that a specific signer (identified by their public key certificate) controlled the content at the time of signing
- Detect any alteration to the signed content by comparing the stored hash to the current hash
- Survive copy-paste, distribution, and format conversion (for text using variation selector embedding)
- Provide cryptographic proof that is verifiable without access to the originating system
- Establish ownership by a human creator, publisher, or organization
- Include machine-readable assertions about AI assistance, rights, and permitted uses

**It cannot do:**
- Mark content it did not have access to at creation time
- Identify content that was not signed (no negative capability)
- Prevent modification - it detects modification after the fact
- Certify the subjective quality or accuracy of content

The key distinction from statistical watermarking is that cryptographic watermarking does not detect. It verifies. Detection is the inference that a pattern exists in unsigned content. Verification is the confirmation that a valid signed manifest is present and unaltered.

## The Durability Difference

One of the practical differences between the two approaches is durability under distribution.

Statistical watermarks in text are fragile to editing because they are statistical properties of token sequences. Substantial editing changes enough tokens that the statistical signal degrades below the detection threshold.

Cryptographic watermarks embedded using variation selectors are durable because the embedding is in the Unicode character sequence, not in the word choices. Copy-paste preserves invisible Unicode characters along with visible characters. HTML rendering preserves them. Email transmission preserves them (for clients that preserve Unicode). Wire services that distribute text over HTTP preserve them.

The durability has limits: systems that strip Unicode to ASCII, some old CMS platforms, and certain PDF conversion paths can remove variation selector characters. This is a known constraint, and Encypher's manifest structure is designed to degrade gracefully, meaning detection of the presence or absence of a manifest is possible even when partial stripping occurs.

Statistical watermarks in images are also relatively fragile to modification. Resizing, re-encoding, adding overlays, or applying filters can degrade or eliminate SynthID's neural network signature.

## Different Questions, Different Audiences

Statistical watermarking answers the question: "Was this content generated by our model?"

This question is useful to the model operator, who wants to track misuse, and potentially to regulators who want to enforce AI content disclosure requirements at the platform level.

It is not useful to a content creator who wants to prove ownership of their work, to a publisher who wants to establish rights claims against an AI training pipeline, or to a media consumer who wants to know whether a piece of journalism was produced by a journalist or generated wholesale by an AI.

Cryptographic watermarking answers the question: "Who created this content, and has it been altered since signing?"

This question is useful to rights holders, publishers, platforms performing provenance verification, regulatory compliance systems that need a machine-readable record of AI assistance, and legal proceedings that require evidence of content origin.

## The [Encypher vs. SynthID Comparison](/compare/encypher-vs-synthid)

A direct comparison is useful here.

SynthID is a Google DeepMind product designed to mark AI-generated outputs from Google's models. It works at the model level, embedded in the generation process. It is not available as a general infrastructure layer for content that human creators produce.

Encypher is content provenance infrastructure for any content, AI-generated or human-created, across 31 media types. It works at the distribution layer, where content is signed before publication. It is available via API to any organization that produces content.

The use cases are structurally different:

SynthID: "We want to know if this text was generated by our model."
Encypher: "We want to prove that our organization created this content and establish a rights record."

SynthID answers a detection question about AI outputs. Encypher answers a provenance question about content ownership. These are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to different questions.

An organization might use both. An AI platform might embed SynthID watermarks in model outputs so the model can identify its own outputs, while also using Encypher signing at the distribution layer to embed C2PA manifests with AI-generated assertions that satisfy Article 50 of the EU AI Act. The two operate at different layers of the content lifecycle.

## When to Use Each Approach

Use statistical watermarking when:
- You are an AI model provider who wants to recognize your own outputs
- The use case is model-level attribution, not content ownership
- Fragility to paraphrasing is acceptable because you are detecting recent, largely unmodified outputs
- You need to scale without per-content signing infrastructure

Use cryptographic watermarking when:
- You are a content creator or publisher establishing ownership
- You need provenance that survives distribution and editing
- You need evidence that holds up to legal challenge
- You are complying with machine-readable marking requirements under Article 50 of the EU AI Act
- You need interoperability with other C2PA-compatible tools and platforms

## The Underlying Principle

The naming confusion between statistical and cryptographic watermarking matters because the two approaches have different failure modes and different evidentiary weights.

A statistical detection result is probabilistic evidence. It can be wrong. It is challengeable by an opposing party who presents evidence that the detected pattern is coincidental or that the paraphrasing threshold is unreliable.

A cryptographic verification result is mathematical evidence. A valid signature from a known key, over a content hash that matches the current content, establishes the fact of signing with the same certainty as any cryptographic proof. Challenging it requires attacking the signature itself or the key's chain of custody, both of which are difficult.

Policy discussions that treat these two approaches as equivalents are making an error that leads to framework designs that appear rigorous but provide only probabilistic enforcement. Machine-readable content provenance requirements, including Article 50 of the EU AI Act, are only as strong as the technical approach used to satisfy them.

Cryptographic watermarking provides a foundation that can bear evidentiary weight. Statistical watermarking provides a signal that is useful but should not be asked to carry more than it can hold.
