# Review Brief for Anne McCarthy and James LePage

## Objective

Show that the Encypher WordPress plugin is more than a plugin integration.

It is a practical demonstration that WordPress can support a standards-based provenance layer for human-authored and AI-assisted publishing.

## The top-line thesis

WordPress needs a provenance and authenticity layer for the AI era.

The Encypher plugin demonstrates one working path:

- install in minutes
- connect a workspace without credential friction
- sign content using open provenance standards
- preserve provenance across edits
- verify publicly on the frontend

## Why this matters to Anne

Anne is likely to care most about:

- interoperability
- open standards
- WordPress-native architecture
- ecosystem extensibility
- whether the concept can benefit the broader WordPress community

### The strongest framing for Anne

Lead with:

- standards-based provenance
- clean WordPress integration
- future ecosystem extensibility
- a provider-neutral architectural direction

Do not lead with:

- monetization mechanics
- legal enforcement language
- coalition economics
- closed-service positioning

## Why this matters to James

James is likely to care most about:

- how this fits AI-assisted publishing
- whether it becomes useful developer infrastructure
- whether agents and future AI tools can preserve provenance
- whether this belongs in a broader WordPress AI platform story

### The strongest framing for James

Lead with:

- provenance for AI-era publishing
- content trust and verification
- compatibility with future editor, agent, and workflow integrations
- developer primitives that other plugins and services can build on

Do not lead with:

- publisher sales strategy
- legal framing as the primary product story
- platform lock-in language

## What to say in the first two minutes

"We think WordPress needs a real provenance and authenticity layer for the AI era. This plugin is our proof that a WordPress-native workflow can make that practical today: install, connect, publish, verify. It uses open standards for proof of origin, preserves provenance through edits, and can evolve beyond a single plugin or provider path."

## The ask

The ask is not:

"Should WordPress adopt Encypher as a required service?"

The ask is:

- does this solve an important WordPress problem
- does the architectural direction fit WordPress extensibility
- what parts should remain plugin territory vs broader platform abstractions
- how should provenance fit the WordPress AI roadmap

## The three moments to land in the meeting

### 1. The install-to-connect moment

The plugin connects from inside WordPress with secure email approval.

This matters because it reduces adoption friction and keeps setup inside a familiar WordPress flow.

### 2. The signed-post moment

A normal publish action results in standards-based proof of origin attached to the post.

This makes provenance feel like a publishing capability, not a security product bolted on from the outside.

### 3. The verification moment

Readers can verify authenticity on the frontend and editors can confirm provenance status inside WordPress.

This is where the feature becomes infrastructure: trust is visible, inspectable, and reusable.

## What success looks like

Success in the meeting is hearing some version of:

- "This feels like a real WordPress problem"
- "This could become a useful primitive for AI-era publishing"
- "This should be thought about as an extensible layer, not just a single plugin"

## What to avoid

Avoid saying:

- "This should obviously go straight into core"
- "The value is mainly legal leverage"
- "The moat is that people need our backend"
- "We already know the answer, we just need a yes"

Instead say:

- "We wanted to prove the workflow concretely"
- "We are interested in the right ecosystem shape"
- "We want to understand what belongs in WordPress, what belongs in plugins, and what belongs in hosted services"
