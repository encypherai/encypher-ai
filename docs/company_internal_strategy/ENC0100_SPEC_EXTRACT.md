GRANULAR CONTENT ATTRIBUTION AND RELATED METHODS AND SYSTEMS 
FOR CONTENT ATTRIBUTION, AUTHENTICATION, AND/OR PROVENANCE 
TECHNICAL FIELD 
[0001] The field of the present disclosure relates to methods and systems for 
encoding electronic content with information regarding attribution, authentication, and/or 
provenance of the content and, in particular, to methods and systems for granularly 
applying attribution, authentication, and/or provenance information to digital text, 
including text streams, for securing such attribution, authentication, and/or provenance 
information to prevent tampering, and for generating evidence of the source, 
authenticity, and/or provenance of digital text. 
BACKGROUND  
[0002] Some existing methods for content validation and attribution involve the 
comparison of entire documents against source documents stored in a trusted 
repository.  Comparison methods may include statistical or linguistic analysis of entire 
documents.  Such methods are inefficient and require source documents to be collected 
in a searchable trusted repository.   
[0003] The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is an industry 
consortium that is developing standards for embedding content provenance and 
attribution information in digital media.  Certain standards proposed by C2PA involve 
hashing an entire digital media asset and encoding the hash into a manifest.  The 
manifest may be securely embedded in the asset (e.g., in the asset file or document) 
and digitally signed to provide secure and largely self-contained information on 
provenance and authenticity.  For digital assets that do not conventionally support

embedding, such as plain text and XML files, C2PA proposes storing their manifests in 
a manifest repository external to the digital assets.  The manifest repository may then 
be queried using a cryptographic hash of the digital asset to determine the asset’s 
provenance and attribution information. 
[0004] The present inventor has recognized that not all digital content is well suited 
for attribution via the C2PA standards.  Moreover, the inventor has recognized that 
when the manifest for a digital asset is stored in a repository separate from the asset, 
any changes to the digital asset that are not recorded in the repository may frustrate the 
ability to find the manifest in the repository. The inventor has also recognized limitations 
with the document-level authentication provided by the C2PA standards and has 
identified a need for a more granular approach to content authentication, which is 
embodied in the methods and systems disclosed herein.  Such a granular approach 
may allow for enhanced provenance and authentication information, partial 
authentication, improved content detection (for example, for plagiarism detection), 
and/or generation of more-detailed evidence of content source and usage, among other 
potential benefits.  The inventor has also identified a need for embedding provenance 
and/or attribution information in content generated by large language models (LLMs) 
and other artificial intelligence (AI) systems, and especially AI-generated content that is 
output as text streams or data streams having a structure and length that is not 
predefined.  The inventor has also identified an opportunity to address the technical 
problem of maintaining content authentication and provenance tracking in environments 
in which a full cryptographic manifest (e.g., C2PA manifest) is impractical due to size 
constraints or due to the active blocking or stripping of such manifests by third-party 
platforms.

[0027] "Attestance payload" means data encoded within text or other content (i.e.,

combinations thereof. 
[0028] "Attestance marker" means an attestance payload comprising a single 
identifier, value, or reference regarding any of authenticity, integrity, source, attribution, 
contextual position, and/or provenance of the content.  An attestance marker is not 
independently structured and typically is not independently signed. Attestance markers 
may include reference markers, integrity markers, positional markers, and attribution 
markers. 
[0029] "Reference marker" means an identifier that references content and/or 
attestance information and/or metadata and/or other data stored at a location  external 
to the content in which the reference marker is embedded. A reference marker may be

[0030] "Source locator" means a reference marker that identifies a network location 
where source content can be accessed. A source locator enables retrieval of the source

[0048] In still other embodiments, in addition to generating the segment hashes, a 
document-level hash value or file-level hash value may be generated from the segment 
hashes by generating a Merkle tree (i.e., calculating a binary hash tree) in a stepwise 
fashion, wherein the root of the Merkle tree is the document-level hash value or file-level 
hash value, as described below with reference to FIG. 3.   
[0049] FIG. 3 illustrates a Merkle tree 300 (binary hash tree) having eight leaf 
hashes (310) HA, HB, …,HH, which are hash values generated from eight text segments 
(312) TA, TB, …,TH, respectively.  The Merkle tree 300 also includes four second-level 
interior hashes (314), HAB, HCD, HEF, HGH, each generated by concatenating their 
constituent leaf hash pairs and then hashing the concatenated number.  For example, if 
hash HA and hash HB are each 256 bit numbers expressed in hexadecimal string format 
by 64 characters each, then appending HB to HA yields a 128 character hexadecimal 
string that is hashed to yield a new 64 character hex number that is interior hash HAB.  
The process is continued to generate additional interior hashes and ultimately the 
Merkle root.  Thus, the Merkle tree 300 also includes two third-level interior hashes 
(316) H
ABCD and HEFGH, each generated by concatenating their constituent second-level 
hashes and hashing the concatenated number; and a Merkle root HABCDEFGH (320) that 
is generated by concatenating the penultimate-level hashes in the tree (third-level in this 
case) HABCD and HEFGH, and hashing the concatenated number. Optionally, a padding 
may be added to an unbalanced tree (e.g. if the quantity of text segments is not a power 
of 2) before further hashing to generate any remaining leaf hashes 310, interior hashes 
314, 316 and Merkle root 320.  While the Merkle tree 300 is illustrated in the context of 
segmented text, in other embodiments a Merkle tree hashing technique could also be 
applied to segmented content of a different type, such as digital audio, video, multi-
media etc.

Alternatively, in some embodiments, the leaf hashes H_A and H_B may be retained in

[0057] Steps 503 through 507 continue this process for additional segments. When a 
new segment hash is generated and no adjacent unpaired hash exists at the same level 
of the Merkle tree (see FIG. 5B), the new hash is stored in the hash buffer. When an

subsequent levels.  This enables the hash buffer size to never exceed O(log n) entries, 
where n is the total number of segments, regardless of content length.

[0105] Turning now to FIG. 15, an embodiment of a robust fingerprinting method 
1500 is illustrated for detecting semantically similar content even when the content has 
been paraphrased, restructured, or partially deleted. Unlike cryptographic hash 
functions that produce completely different outputs for any input change, a fingerprinting 
method employs locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) to generate fingerprints (fingerprint 
hashes) that remain similar for similar content. 
[0106] In one embodiment, the method 1500 for fingerprint hash generation 
(performed at signing time) proceeds as follows.  At step 1510, the content is 
preprocessed by converting it to lowercase, removing punctuation, and applying 
stemming or lemmatization to reduce words to their base forms. At step 1520, n-gram 
shingles are extracted from the preprocessed content. In one embodiment, 3-word 
shingles are used, wherein a sliding window of three consecutive words generates 
overlapping shingles (e.g., "the quick brown", "quick brown fox", etc.). 
[0107] At step 1530, a locality-sensitive hash (LSH) is computed from the shingle 
set. Examples of suitable LSH algorithms include MinHash, SimHash, or random 
hyperplane projection. The resulting fingerprint is a compact representation (e.g., in the 
range of 64 to 256 bits) that captures the semantic essence of the content. At step 
1540, the fingerprint is optionally embedded in the content using the distributed variation 
selector encoding (see discussion of FIG. 13) with high redundancy to maximize 
survival across modifications and/or stored in an external database or hash table 
indexed by the fingerprint value.   
[0108] In some embodiments, fingerprints are generated for each text segment (such 
as each sentence or paragraph) and a Merkle root is calculated for the complete text, by 
combining and hashing adjacent pairs of fingerprints (e.g. using a cryptographic hash 
function) to form a Merkle tree. This hybrid fingerprinting and Merkle tree approach may 
provide the benefits of: (1) fuzzy matching for similar text segments, (2) document-level 
authentication via the Merkle root, (3) segment fingerprint integrity via Merkle proofs 
(i.e., a way to verify that a specific embedded fingerprint hasn't been tampered with), 
and (4) computational efficiency for building the document-level hash, and (5) 
compatibility with streaming applications. A potential the trade-off of this approach 
(relative to cryptographic hashing of segments) is a loss of the ability to verify segment-

level text integrity. In other words, this approach lacks a mechanism to verify that a 
target text exactly matches the source text, since similar text segments could produce 
the same fingerprints by design. For exact text verification at the segment level, 
cryptographic hashes of the segments would be required.  So, in some embodiments, 
both fingerprint hashes and cryptographic hashes may be generated for each segment, 
and then used to generate a Merkle tree or two related Merkle trees. 
[0109] Detection (performed at verification time) proceeds as follows. At step 1550, 
embedded fingerprints are extracted by scanning for patterns of non-rendering Unicode 
code points in the text, indicating embedded attestance payload, and, from the 
attestance payload, retrieving fingerprint hash data, if any. The text segment 
corresponding to the fingerprint hash can be re-hashed using the same fingerprinting 
hashing technique as used with the source text, to verify that the text hasn’t been 
significantly changed relative to the embedded fingerprint and that the fingerprint hasn’t 
been corrupted. If a fingerprint hash is not embedded, then a new fingerprint hash can 
be generated by segmenting, normalizing, and hashing according to the fingerprinting 
method, for querying a database or other data repository.  At step 1560, the extracted 
fingerprint or a newly generated fingerprint of the target text (if different or if a fingerprint 
is not embedded in the target text) is compared against a database of known 
fingerprints via a similarity analysis.  A similarity analysis technique may use a similarity 
metric such as Jaccard similarity, cosine similarity, or Hamming distance. The 
comparison yields a similarity score indicating how closely the target content matches 
known content. At step 1570, a match decision may be made based on whether the 
similarity score exceeds a predetermined threshold. If a match is found, the output 
includes the source document identifier and confidence level. 
[0110] The robust fingerprinting method 1500 provides tolerance for various content 
modifications. Paraphrasing changes some shingles but preserves others, maintaining a 
detectable similarity. Restructuring such as sentence reordering is tolerated because 
shingles within sentences are preserved. Partial deletion leaves remaining text 
matchable. Combination of original content with other content still permits detection of 
the original portions.
