### Official Color Palette

**IMPORTANT: These colors are fixed and should NOT be changed. Our logo uses Deep Navy as the primary color.**

**Primary Colors:**
- **Deep Navy (#1B2F50):** Our signature color from the logo. Use for backgrounds, headers, and text on light backgrounds. Conveys security, professionalism, and trust. Should dominate large areas like banners and footers.
- **Azure Blue (#2A87C4):** Our primary brand highlight color from the logo (the "AI" text). Use for buttons, links, CTAs, and any element that needs to stand out. Signals innovation and clarity.
- **Light Sky Blue (#B7D5ED):** Secondary backgrounds, infographics, hover states. Provides soft contrast against navy. Used in the logo behind the padlock icon. Creates openness and breathing room.

**Accent & Supporting Colors:**
- **Cyber Teal (#00CED1):** Use sparingly for security-focused highlights, alerts, or important notifications. Aligns with cybersecurity industry standards while providing strong contrast. Use in charts, icons, or to draw attention to critical information.
- **Neutral Gray (#A7AFBC):** Body text, secondary text, strokes, and lines. Use instead of pure black for a softer, modern look. Ensures readability while maintaining approachable tone.
- **Pure White (#FFFFFF):** Backgrounds and whitespace. Essential for clarity and cleanliness. Generous white spacing communicates transparency and keeps content uncluttered.

**Typography:**
- **Primary Font:** Roboto (all weights)
- **Headings:** Roboto Bold or Medium
- **Body Text:** Roboto Regular
- **Code/Technical:** Roboto Mono

**Color Usage Guidelines:**
- Deep Navy: 40-50% of design (backgrounds, headers, primary text)
- Azure Blue: 20-30% (CTAs, links, highlights)
- Light Sky Blue: 15-20% (secondary backgrounds, subtle accents)
- Cyber Teal: 5-10% (critical alerts, security-focused elements)
- Neutral Gray: Body text and supporting elements
- White: Generous whitespace throughout

**Never Use:**
- Pure black (#000000) - too harsh
- Bright red - too aggressive for our collaborative tone
- Neon colors - unprofessional
- Colors outside this palette without executive approval

### vs. Wait and See:
"Standards markets converge quickly. The publisher coalition implementing marked content creates network effects. Founding members shape market licensing frameworks through the roundtable track. Later adopters accept terms others defined."

### vs. Tier 1 Licensing / Access Gates (TollBit, Cloudflare, RSL):
"TollBit and Cloudflare are Tier 1 licensing tools -- access gates that AI developers have to opt into. Valuable when AI companies cooperate, but no visibility when they don't. Our provenance is embedded in the content itself -- no AI company cooperation required. Complementary: access gates for cooperative AI companies, Encypher for enforcement against everyone else."

### vs. Output Attribution (ProRata, Dappier):
"ProRata and Dappier are also opt-in systems -- attribution only works when AI companies voluntarily integrate. No opt-in, no attribution. Our cryptographic provenance is embedded before content enters any AI system and works regardless of cooperation. One requires participation. The other works unilaterally."

### vs. Big Tech Marketplaces (Microsoft PCM):
"Microsoft is simultaneously the largest enterprise AI buyer of publisher content and the marketplace operator. We're independent infrastructure -- we don't buy content, run AI models, or compete with publishers. Our provenance works across every AI company, not locked to one marketplace."

### vs. AI-Output Watermarking (SynthID):
"SynthID marks AI output. We mark human content. Opposite problems. SynthID's statistical watermarking is academically fragile under paraphrasing. Our cryptographic embedding is deterministic."

### vs. Blockchain Timestamping (WordProof):
"Timestamping proves content existed. Provenance proves ownership wherever it appears. WordProof's proof is external and doesn't travel with the text. Ours is embedded and survives distribution."

**When Prospects Mention Adjacent Players:**
The market is forming around three layers: (1) Tier 1 licensing / access control -- TollBit, Cloudflare AI Audit, RSL -- opt-in access gates that AI developers must voluntarily participate in, (2) Content provenance -- Encypher -- works unilaterally, no AI company cooperation required, and (3) Attribution/monetization -- ProRata, Dappier, Microsoft PCM -- opt-in systems requiring AI company integration. We are the only company at Layer 2 for text, and the only layer that works without AI company cooperation. Frame adjacent players as complementary where possible. Key distinction: Layers 1 and 3 only work when AI developers opt in; Layer 2 works regardless.

## The "Willful Infringement" Messaging Framework (NEW)

### The Legal Transformation:

**Current State (Without Encypher):**
- Publisher: "You trained on our content"
- AI Company: "Prove it. Also, we can't control what users paste. We didn't know it was yours."
- Result: Expensive discovery, uncertain outcomes, "innocent infringement" defense viable

**With Encypher Infrastructure:**
- Publisher embeds persistent cryptographic signatures at sentence level
- Publisher formally notifies AI Company: "Our content is marked. You can verify via public API."
- AI Company choices:
 - License properly "
 - Ignore notification (negligent)
 - Strip signatures (willful)
- Publisher: "We notified you. Here's proof the signatures were there and you ignored them."
- Result: Willful infringement = materially stronger posture (especially for registered works)

- "Eliminate the 'we didn't know' defense"
- "Transform innocent infringement into willful infringement"
- "Formal notice + cryptographic proof = registration-qualified statutory leverage"
