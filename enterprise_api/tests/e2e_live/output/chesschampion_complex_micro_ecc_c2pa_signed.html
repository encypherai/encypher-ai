1󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆖󠇭︉󠆭󠅁󠇎󠄿󠄷󠆧󠆳󠄊󠇊󠄰︂󠄧󠄋󠆍󠆱󠆙󠅂󠄼󠆤󠆛󠆲󠆶󠇁󠇢󠅷󠅙︀󠄴󠄈󠄗󠆮󠅆󠇉󠇑󠆞󠆺󠄰. Garry Kasparov vs. Veslin Topalov

Possibly the most famous and tactical chess game in history, the January 1999 game between Garry Kasparov and Veslin Topalov has gone down in history as a strategic masterpiece worthy of study󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇣󠅡󠅯󠇆󠅓󠆶󠄽󠄄󠆩󠄞󠄰󠅉󠅠󠆞󠆲󠆫󠅄󠇠󠅤󠅒󠅐󠆞󠄃󠆌󠄼󠄆󠅩︅󠅬󠆈󠄎󠄞󠄀󠆴󠅎󠅨󠆊󠄮󠅣󠇊. Occurring at the 1999 Hoogovens Tournament (now the Tata Steel Chess Tournament), the game occurred at the height of Garry Kasparov’s chess career, when he was both reigning world champion and had recently endured a historic match against the chess computer Deep Blue󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄬󠅭󠄄󠆑󠇝󠆄󠄰󠄶󠆄󠄹︎︆󠄅󠇙󠅱󠆕󠅰󠇮󠅃󠆆󠅘󠄘󠆎󠆿󠆽󠆻󠇊󠅇󠄯󠇝︄︂󠄕󠄉󠇥󠄭󠆝︋󠇮󠇏.

The game, later known as “Kasparov’s Immortal,” began with a classic king’s pawn opening, but immediately, Topalov put Kasparov on his toes with an unorthodox Pirc Defense, moving his queen pawn to d6󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆷󠄹󠅦󠅙󠆒󠇦󠄸︄󠆃󠅪󠅶󠇛󠆮󠆤󠇢󠅖󠅘󠆡󠄪󠇦󠆴󠆛󠆸󠇀󠅗󠇦󠆆󠄜󠆏󠆈󠆗󠆤󠄓󠄯󠅩󠇎󠅬󠇗󠅋󠆥. From there, the game became a slow tactical defense, with both players castling queenside and very little occurring in the first two dozen moves󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇐󠇏󠆟󠇣󠇃󠄢󠄳󠆇󠅼󠄴󠆟󠅢︋󠅉︀󠅟󠆉󠇓️󠆉󠅆︂󠄍󠅲󠅖󠅏󠆋󠄩󠄿󠄺󠄛󠄳󠇉󠅫󠆂󠅅󠅹󠇡󠆵󠅧. However, on turnset 24, Kasparov made the first of his brilliant but risky rook sacrifices, of which there would be three in the game󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇯󠆖󠄃󠄌️󠄋󠄽󠅞󠆠󠄟︀󠆊󠅺󠆥󠇨󠄕󠆈︀󠅠󠅿󠅆󠅸󠄸󠇖󠆉󠅋︇󠆙󠅊󠆴󠅜󠆡󠆝󠆎󠇝󠆅󠆗󠆣󠆽󠄜. Kasparov’s sacrifices paid off, as Topalov was drawn into a trap that forced him to move his king the full length of the board, eventually leading him to resign on the 44th move, causing the audience of spectators to break out in applause at Kasparov’s victory󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇒󠅟󠇎󠄜󠄛󠆴󠄴󠆣󠆘󠄝󠅪󠇠󠆉󠅭󠅥󠅅󠄽󠄃󠇠󠅾󠅣󠆜󠄀󠆩󠇒󠇚󠄿󠅛󠅳󠆗󠅉󠇀󠆏󠅆󠅞󠄵󠆴󠄏󠄖󠅔.

2󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢︁󠄆󠄩󠄳︇󠇯󠄸󠇍󠆡󠅊󠆝󠇚󠅈󠅟󠄬󠆙󠆚󠆏󠆉󠇯󠅋󠅎󠇍󠄲󠆔󠅨󠅱󠄲󠇄󠇝󠅑󠄉󠆳󠅣󠅄󠅼󠄓󠅘󠅮󠆴. Bobby Fischer vs. Daniel Byrne

In 1956, a chess game occurred which was referred to as “the Game of the Century,” a name which has stuck with it in the nearly 100 years since it was played󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄬󠅱󠆃󠅍︎󠄨󠄴󠅁󠆅󠅺󠆘󠆔󠆿󠇘󠆍󠅚󠇋󠆳󠆊󠄦󠅜󠅢󠇒󠄟󠅬︃󠅋󠄪󠄆󠅖󠅢󠇭󠄈󠆬󠄢󠄘󠇡󠅾󠄼󠆈. This game featured one of the leading American chess masters of the day, Daniel Byrne, facing off against a rising star in the chess world, a 13-year-old boy named Bobby Fischer󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄳󠆁󠅄󠇫󠆀󠄛󠄽󠅚󠆝󠇓󠄡󠄫︈󠇎󠇐󠄓󠄕󠅢󠇢󠇟󠆺󠅊󠄑󠆺󠇘︍󠆧󠆨󠄎󠅮󠄡󠄄󠅞󠆚󠄮󠄝󠆟󠅔󠅲󠆠. Nowadays, most chess fanatics know Bobby Fischer as one of the greatest chess players of all time, a genius and natural prodigy, but it was in the Game of the Century that Fischer first alerted the chess world to his natural talents󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄭︎󠄳󠅅󠄓󠇃󠄸󠄖󠆄󠆑󠄻󠇌󠆔󠆳︃󠄧󠇛󠇈󠆲󠅣︃󠄍󠄿󠅻󠄣󠆐󠅧󠅀󠇀󠅱󠄌󠅚󠆩󠄳󠅤󠅢󠆖󠇮󠄾󠅛.

Occurring at the Rosenwald Memorial Tournament in New York City, this game began with Byrne, playing white, making a safe move with one of his knights󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆤󠆡󠇧󠄄︅︌󠄷󠄧󠆘︍󠆶︃󠅯󠇈󠆝󠅤󠅹󠄞󠆹󠆴󠆚󠆬󠆧󠇭󠆟󠄲󠇧󠅔󠅊󠄽󠅧󠄋󠇀󠆡󠇍󠇬󠄓󠇓󠇟󠄍. Fischer used a hypermodern defense, hoping to draw out Byrne’s pawns to the center so he could target them with his major pieces󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇛󠆞󠆈️󠇨󠄉󠄿󠆾󠆉󠆀󠆮󠆬󠆕󠇕󠆯󠅗󠅕󠄈󠇬󠆫󠆇󠄙󠇦󠄙󠅮󠄁󠄐󠆮󠇎󠆷󠇃󠇧︇󠆀󠇀󠅋󠄋󠇟󠅘󠄦. Byrne defended cautiously, but made a few minor mistakes, and on his 11th move, Fischer made a stunning knight sacrifice, which, even though Byrne did not take it, caused many spectators at other tables to make their way over to watch󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢︌󠆄󠆞︎󠆡󠄶󠄹󠆩󠆙󠄍󠇐︈󠄉󠄀󠇊󠆐󠆻󠅊󠅭󠇏󠅞󠆤︉󠄪󠄺󠅉󠆓󠄿󠅚󠄽󠄠󠅒󠅟󠆓󠇏︄󠇉󠅗󠅆󠆯. The most famous move of the game came on Fischer’s 17th move, when he stunningly offered to sacrifice his queen󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠅁󠄭󠇛󠇋󠇖󠄧󠄵󠅬󠆖󠇋󠇌󠅃󠅰󠄆︃󠅌󠅗󠆎󠆪󠅛󠇎󠅙󠅯󠅒󠆲󠅿󠅍󠆇󠆬󠄏󠅥󠇯󠅔󠆵󠆠󠆜󠅝󠆔󠅸󠅏. Byrne took the bait, but in doing so, he left Fischer with too much material󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇤󠅶󠆊󠆤󠇣󠄇󠄶󠆚󠆜󠅹󠆤󠇌󠅶󠇭󠅂󠅷󠆜󠅗󠆉󠅋󠇤󠇦󠇯󠄾󠅵󠆡󠇧󠇧󠄁󠄻󠅢󠇜󠄲󠄗󠅶︌󠆻󠅂󠄡󠆪. Ultimately, Fischer won the match with a checkmate, defeating a seasoned master at just 13 and kickstarting his legendary career󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆗︉󠄯󠆹󠄃󠅧󠄳󠅦󠆜󠅝󠅇󠆸󠇣󠆢󠅃󠄛󠆦󠆗󠅃︇󠆩󠄹󠇇󠇅󠆩󠄢󠇯󠇑󠆆󠆷󠅱󠄰󠅇󠆝󠇊󠆷󠆯󠅺󠆮󠆠.

3. Levon Aronian vs. Viswanathan Anand

Known as “Anand’s Immortal,” this 2013 game between reigning world champion Viswanathan Anand and Levon Aronian at the Tata Steel Chess Tournament is one of the greatest games ever played󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄎󠇏󠄞󠇄󠅩󠆼󠄺󠆗󠆂󠆼󠇦󠆿󠄞󠄊󠄃󠇬︌󠄙󠅭󠅎󠅺󠄇󠅓󠆌󠅮󠄚󠅔󠄾󠅷󠆨󠄂󠇏󠄺󠇔󠆲󠆶󠅝󠅰󠇫󠅻. The game occurred ahead of the scheduled championship match between Anand and Magnus Carlsen, where Anand would ultimately lose his title, but it stands out nonetheless as an absolute masterpiece of tactical maneuvering󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆊︈󠇓󠆱󠅯󠆏󠄾󠅭󠆮󠄪󠆨󠄻󠆄󠆘󠄥󠆋󠅧󠆂󠄚󠄍󠅒󠄢󠇛󠄊󠄍󠇫󠅂󠅊󠇑󠅉󠇅󠄜󠇔󠄽󠇈󠅌󠆜󠇐󠆉󠆒.

The game opened with a Meran Variation on the Semi-Slav Defense, with both players advancing their queen pawns󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇈󠄔󠆖󠅗󠇫󠆑󠄹󠇥󠆐󠆀󠆸󠄶󠆁󠄦󠇓󠆲󠅟󠅥󠇤󠄉󠄑󠇂󠅽󠄥󠅲󠄑󠄔󠇤󠇬󠅴󠆫︅󠅑󠆎󠄊󠆰󠄺󠄝󠅘󠆰. Up until the 12th move, the game was a standard back-and-forth of well-practiced opening moves and responses󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠆧󠄖󠆈󠄒󠄏󠅳󠄵󠇙󠅴󠆸󠅭󠄁󠄏︀󠅟󠄳󠆜󠆾󠆝󠄘󠄅󠄎󠆣󠆙󠆇󠄪󠇣󠄡󠇘󠅠󠆔󠄐󠄗󠅊󠄬󠄾󠅌󠄰󠅮󠇐. It was in the 12th move that things began to open up, with Anand offering a pawn sacrifice that Aronian accepted, and Aronian making a key pawn error on the 14th move󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠅟󠇡󠄏󠆸󠆦󠆺󠄾󠄤󠆨󠄄󠄔󠅐️󠄲󠅵󠅗󠇠󠄅󠆫󠇠󠆙󠆾󠄤󠄊󠆥󠇇󠄵󠆅󠄻︊󠄟󠅯󠅶󠅵󠆯󠆒󠅠󠆣󠆭󠄜. Anand would secure victory in this game by offering a few key sacrifices, including a rook, to create a combination that ultimately left Aronian with no way to escape, forcing him to resign on the 23rd move󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄅󠆼󠄩󠅳󠅴󠇒󠄲󠇧󠆭󠅟󠄅󠇗󠇍󠄵󠄙󠅸󠅠󠅪󠄋󠆫󠇒󠅉󠄹󠄟󠅋︊︉󠄴󠆆󠄰󠄊󠆗󠆚󠆌󠇑󠆉󠇠󠆢󠇐󠄵.

4. Paul Morphy vs. Allies

This extremely interesting game from 1858 has cemented itself in history as one of the most fascinating and instructive displays of chess talent in the world󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢︅󠅕󠅣󠅮󠅏󠇎󠄿󠅎󠆏󠅈󠅜󠅵󠆗󠄫󠆐󠄲󠄳󠅞󠆊󠄁󠆡󠅜󠆌󠄇󠅹󠅝󠇂󠇔󠄄󠅍󠅦󠅍󠆽󠅇󠇩󠆍󠆬󠅜󠆷︄. Known as the “Opera Game” due to its being played at a Parisian opera house, it featured American chess master Paul Morphy playing a consultation game against two opponents: German nobleman Karl II, Duke of Brunswick, and French aristocrat Comte Isouard de Vauvenargues󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄂󠄜󠆋󠇔󠄎󠅴󠄺󠅶󠆘󠇤󠆰󠇭󠄊󠄘󠇩󠄷󠄤︄󠅸󠆃󠆍󠆗󠅢󠆡󠆽󠅙󠆑󠆐󠄃󠄨󠅿󠆣󠇠󠆦󠄒󠇉󠇟󠅅󠇪󠅗. While Morphy played solo, Karl II and Comte Isouard decided jointly which moves to make on each of their shared turns󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠅚󠇡󠄞󠄙󠇗󠇡󠄽󠅖󠆁󠇅󠆞󠅜︁󠆒󠄏󠅸󠇑󠅚󠆺󠇛󠆉󠄩󠅰󠄮󠄉󠆋󠇓󠅵󠅓󠇗󠅮󠇈︎󠄨󠆕󠄝󠅢󠄆︇󠆬.

The Opera Game is often used as an instructional game for chess players because it illustrates valuable concepts such as piece development, control of the center, and the wise use of sacrifices󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠇩󠆈󠇏󠆟󠅊️󠄶󠄟󠅽󠇇󠅼󠄫󠄬󠅘󠅌󠇧󠇝󠄇󠄷󠄼󠆕󠄳󠅧󠆌󠇈󠄴󠄔󠅷󠅗󠇅󠄁󠅑󠇯󠅯󠅺󠆡󠇉󠄎󠄤󠅦. Despite having two brains on their side, both of whom were decently-skilled chess players themselves, the Allies (Karl II and Isouard) proved unable to defeat Morphy, in fact losing quite quickly󠇟󠇠󠇡󠇢󠄱󠅴️󠆬󠄢󠆘󠄹󠄆󠆞󠇂󠄾󠅰󠇄󠆽󠆆󠆋󠇁󠅃󠅲︃️󠄸󠅞󠆐󠅽󠅁󠅉︀󠄑󠄵󠆅󠅰󠅆󠆺󠆛󠆗󠅽󠅆󠇣󠇃.
